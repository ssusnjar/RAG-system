# Travel and Business Trip Policy

## Purpose

This document defines the rules for planning, approving, booking, and reimbursing business travel undertaken on behalf of the company.

The purpose of this policy is to ensure that business travel is:
- necessary and appropriately approved
- planned in a cost-conscious and operationally sensible way
- booked through the right channels where available
- aligned with the company’s expense control expectations
- documented well enough to support reimbursement and review

Business travel can be necessary for client delivery, internal coordination, business development, events, training, or other approved company purposes. At the same time, travel creates cost, scheduling, and compliance considerations. This policy is intended to balance operational flexibility with financial discipline.

This document should help employees, managers, and approvers understand:
- when travel needs approval
- how travel should be booked
- what travel class and accommodation standards apply
- how travel-related expenses interact with the Expense Reimbursement and Approval Policy
- what this policy does and does not cover

## Scope

This policy applies to:
- full-time employees
- part-time employees
- fixed-term employees
- interns, where travel is approved as part of their role
- contractors or consultants only where travel is explicitly approved under their engagement terms

This policy applies to company-related travel such as:
- client visits
- internal meetings requiring travel
- project-related travel
- conference or event attendance
- business development meetings
- approved training trips

This policy covers:
- approval requirements
- booking expectations
- travel class standards
- accommodation expectations
- selected reimbursement-related travel rules
- non-compliance handling

This policy does not define in detail:
- immigration or visa procedures
- relocation support
- personal travel extensions
- exact daily allowance values
- local tax treatment
- all reimbursement documentation rules
- every possible transportation scenario
- safety and emergency procedures for all countries

Expense submission mechanics, approval thresholds for expense claims, and reimbursement timelines are primarily governed by the Expense Reimbursement and Approval Policy.

## Core principles

### Business necessity

Travel should only be undertaken where there is a legitimate business reason. Travel should not be treated as the default option where a remote meeting or another practical alternative would reasonably achieve the same result.

Before planning travel, employees and managers should consider:
- whether the trip is necessary
- whether the timing is appropriate
- whether the objective could be achieved remotely
- whether multiple travel purposes can be combined efficiently

### Advance approval

Travel should be approved before booking unless an exceptional situation makes prior approval impractical. The approval requirement exists to ensure that the company reviews necessity, timing, and expected cost before a commitment is made.

### Cost-conscious planning

Travel arrangements should reflect reasonable cost awareness. Employees are expected to select practical options rather than premium options unless a justified exception has been approved.

### Reasonable comfort, not premium by default

The company expects travel to be practical and workable, but not luxurious by default. Travel choices should support the business purpose without unnecessary extra cost.

### Policy alignment

Travel approval alone does not override other policies. Even when a trip is approved:
- expense claims still need proper documentation
- reimbursement still depends on compliance with the Expense Policy
- premium travel choices may still require additional approval
- non-compliant expenses may still be rejected

## Approval requirements

### International travel

All international business travel requires manager approval before booking.

This requirement applies regardless of whether the anticipated cost is low or high. The company expects managerial review before an employee commits to international travel arrangements.

International travel should not be booked first and justified later except in genuinely exceptional circumstances.

### Domestic travel

Domestic travel should also be approved in advance unless it is explicitly covered by a standing team arrangement.

A standing team arrangement may apply in cases where:
- a team regularly travels between specific locations
- recurring site visits are already known and approved in principle
- the manager has clearly documented that advance approval is not required for a specific repeated travel pattern

If no such standing arrangement exists, domestic travel should be approved before booking.

### Additional approval considerations

Even when manager approval is the baseline requirement, additional review may be appropriate where:
- the trip is unusually expensive
- premium travel class is requested
- the trip includes unusual accommodation cost
- the trip has weak or unclear business justification
- the travel is combined with a conference, event, or hospitality activity requiring additional budget scrutiny

This policy does not define a separate universal travel-budget approval table. Where cost sensitivity is high, the manager should involve Finance or the relevant budget owner as appropriate.

## Travel planning expectations

Employees should plan travel with enough lead time to:
- obtain approval
- secure reasonable prices
- coordinate schedules
- avoid last-minute premium bookings where possible
- ensure that any needed client or internal meetings are confirmed

Poor planning does not automatically make higher-cost travel reimbursable.

Employees should provide enough context for approval, including where relevant:
- purpose of the trip
- destination
- dates
- expected meetings or event
- business reason the trip is necessary
- expected major costs if known

## Booking rules

Employees should use approved booking channels when available.

Approved booking channels may include:
- internal travel booking support
- designated agency channels
- approved self-booking platforms
- manager-approved booking methods used by the company

Where approved channels exist, employees should not routinely bypass them without a reason.

### Why approved channels matter

Using approved channels helps the company:
- maintain cost visibility
- support travel recordkeeping
- apply preferred rates where available
- assist with changes or disruptions
- reduce ad hoc reimbursement issues

### Exceptions to channel use

An alternative booking route may be acceptable where:
- no approved channel is reasonably available
- urgent travel is required
- the approved route cannot meet the operational need in time
- an approved manager or operations contact authorizes another method

When an exception is used, the employee should retain clear documentation.

## Transportation standards

### Flights

Economy class is the default for flights.

Business class requires explicit pre-approval from Finance and the department head.

This means that:
- manager approval alone is not sufficient for business class
- business class should not be booked by default based on seniority alone
- convenience or personal preference alone is not sufficient justification

The company may consider exceptions where there is a strong business or operational reason, but those exceptions must be approved in advance.

### Rail and other ground transport

Employees should select reasonable rail or ground transportation options aligned with the purpose and duration of the trip.

Premium rail classes or premium transfer options should be avoided unless:
- the cost difference is small and operationally justified
- there is no practical standard alternative
- the option has been approved where needed

### Local transportation

Reasonable local transportation costs may be reimbursable when directly connected to approved business travel. Employees should use practical judgment and avoid unnecessary premium options.

## Accommodation

Hotel selection should be reasonable in price and location relative to the business purpose.

When choosing accommodation, employees should consider:
- proximity to the meeting or work location
- safety and practicality
- availability
- overall trip cost
- whether a lower-cost comparable option exists nearby

The company does not require the absolute cheapest option in every case, but it does expect reasonable judgment.

Accommodation that is clearly excessive relative to the trip purpose may be challenged or only partially reimbursed.

## Meals and incidental costs during travel

Reasonable meal and incidental costs incurred during approved business travel may be reimbursable, subject to the Expense Reimbursement and Approval Policy.

Employees should exercise moderation and retain documentation where required.

This policy does not define exact per diem or daily allowance values. Daily allowances may apply according to the applicable internal finance rules, but exact allowance values are not defined in this document.

Employees should not assume that all incidental travel spending is automatically reimbursable simply because they were traveling.

## Relationship to the Expense Policy

Travel-related expenses must be submitted according to the Expense Reimbursement and Approval Policy submission deadline.

This means, among other things:
- travel-related claims must be submitted within 15 calendar days of the expense date
- travel-related expenses still require receipts or invoices where required
- approval thresholds in the Expense Policy still apply to expense claims
- travel-related non-compliance may lead to reimbursement rejection

This policy governs whether and how travel should be approved and booked. The Expense Policy governs how the resulting expenses are submitted, reviewed, and reimbursed.

### Examples of interaction

- A trip may be manager-approved, but a premium travel upgrade may still be rejected if it did not receive the extra approval required by this policy.
- A business trip may be valid, but if the employee submits the hotel cost late or without documentation, reimbursement may still be delayed or rejected under the Expense Policy.
- A travel expense may fall into a higher expense approval threshold even when the trip itself was already approved.

## Conferences, events, and training travel

Travel for conferences, events, and training should be approved based on business relevance, expected value, and cost.

Approval should consider:
- relevance to the employee’s role
- expected outcome or business benefit
- total estimated cost
- whether attendance is necessary in person
- whether a lower-cost alternative exists

Approval to attend an event does not automatically approve premium travel or unrelated hospitality costs.

## Combined personal and business travel

This policy does not fully define all scenarios involving mixed personal and business travel.

As a general rule:
- only the business-related portion may be reimbursable
- additional personal costs should not be charged to the company
- arrangements that materially increase company cost for personal convenience may be rejected unless approved in advance

Where travel includes a personal extension or mixed-purpose itinerary, the employee should seek clarification before booking.

## Changes, cancellations, and disruptions

Travel plans sometimes change due to client needs, operational changes, transport disruption, or other issues.

Where possible, employees should:
- notify the manager promptly of significant changes
- choose a reasonable rebooking option
- document major disruption-related costs
- avoid unnecessary premium rebooking unless justified and approved

Reasonable costs caused by genuine business or travel disruption may still be reimbursable, but they remain subject to documentation and review.

## Non-compliance

Unapproved premium travel bookings may be partially or fully rejected for reimbursement.

Other examples of non-compliance may include:
- booking international travel without manager approval
- booking business class without Finance and department head approval
- selecting clearly excessive accommodation without justification
- bypassing approved booking channels without reason
- failing to provide documentation needed for reimbursement
- assuming trip approval overrides the Expense Policy

Non-compliance does not automatically mean the trip had no business purpose, but it may still affect reimbursement outcome.

## Responsibilities

### Employee responsibilities

Employees are responsible for:
- confirming that the trip has a valid business purpose
- obtaining required approval before booking
- booking through approved channels where available
- choosing reasonable travel and accommodation options
- retaining documentation for reimbursement
- submitting travel-related expenses according to the Expense Policy
- raising questions in advance when a travel scenario is unusual

### Manager responsibilities

Managers are responsible for:
- deciding whether travel is necessary
- reviewing whether the trip is justified and timely
- considering expected cost and business value
- ensuring that recurring travel patterns are clearly communicated if standing approval exists
- escalating unusual or high-cost travel where additional review is appropriate

### Finance responsibilities

Finance is responsible where this policy specifically requires Finance involvement, such as business class pre-approval, and may also review travel-related reimbursement issues under the Expense Policy.

Finance is not required to approve a premium travel request merely because the trip itself is business-related.

## Exceptions

Exceptions to this policy should be limited and should be justified by operational need.

Examples may include:
- urgent international travel requiring accelerated booking
- last-minute rebooking caused by transport disruption
- lack of practical standard-class availability
- exceptional operational circumstances affecting safety, timing, or continuity

Exceptions should generally be documented and, where applicable, approved in advance.

An exception should not be treated as a general entitlement for future trips.

## Common mistakes to avoid

The following are common travel-policy mistakes:
- assuming international travel can be booked first and approved later
- confusing trip approval with expense reimbursement approval
- booking business class with only manager approval
- choosing accommodation based on preference rather than reasonableness
- failing to use approved booking channels when available
- assuming all incidental costs are automatically reimbursable
- ignoring the Expense Policy submission deadline
- treating standing domestic approval as if it applies to every travel scenario
