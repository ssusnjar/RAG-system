# Incident Management and Reporting Procedure

## Purpose

This document defines how employees and contractors should identify, report, and respond to incidents affecting company systems, data, operations, or security.

The purpose of this procedure is to ensure that:
- incidents are reported quickly
- the right people are informed
- incidents are handled in a structured and coordinated way
- damage and operational disruption are minimized
- incidents are documented and reviewed
- security and data incidents are handled appropriately

This procedure applies to all employees and contractors who use company systems, devices, or data.

This procedure should be read together with:
- Security and Access Provisioning Policy
- Data Access and Data Handling Policy
- Offboarding Policy (for access removal scenarios)
- Project procedures where incidents affect client work

## What is an incident

An incident is any event that:
- disrupts normal operations
- causes data loss or data exposure
- affects system availability
- affects system integrity
- involves unauthorized access
- involves loss or theft of company equipment
- causes major project disruption
- involves accidental data sharing
- involves security concerns
- causes significant financial or operational impact
- requires urgent coordination to resolve

Not every small issue is an incident, but if there is uncertainty, it is better to report the situation and let it be classified.

### Examples of incidents

Examples include:
- lost or stolen company laptop
- company account compromised
- client data accidentally sent to the wrong person
- production system outage
- major project system failure
- unauthorized access detected
- data deleted accidentally
- critical service unavailable
- security vulnerability discovered in production
- ransomware or malware detected
- employee accidentally publishes confidential data
- backup failure discovered
- important system misconfiguration
- accidental deletion of production database records

### Examples of non-incident issues

Examples of issues that are normally not incidents:
- minor bugs
- small delays in project tasks
- normal system errors without operational impact
- personal device issues not affecting company data
- low-priority support requests

However, if an issue becomes large enough to affect operations or data, it may become an incident.

## Incident severity levels

Incidents can be classified into severity levels to determine urgency and response.

### High severity incidents

High severity incidents typically include:
- production system outage
- data breach or suspected data breach
- loss of client data
- unauthorized access to production systems
- ransomware or malware affecting company systems
- major client-facing system failure
- loss of critical infrastructure
- incident with legal or contractual impact
- incident affecting many users or clients
- incident requiring immediate management involvement

High severity incidents require immediate reporting and response.

### Medium severity incidents

Medium severity incidents may include:
- partial system outage
- limited data exposure
- access granted incorrectly
- important system misconfiguration
- loss of non-critical device
- project system outage affecting one project
- accidental deletion of non-critical data
- repeated system failure affecting work

Medium severity incidents should be reported and handled promptly but may not require emergency response.

### Low severity incidents

Low severity incidents may include:
- minor access issue
- small data mistake quickly corrected
- temporary tool outage with workaround
- minor security concern without confirmed impact

Low severity incidents should still be reported if they involve data, access, or security concerns.

## Incident reporting

### When to report

Employees must report an incident immediately if:
- company device is lost or stolen
- account credentials may be compromised
- confidential or client data may have been exposed
- production system is down
- unauthorized access is suspected
- major system error occurs
- important data is deleted accidentally
- security vulnerability is discovered
- ransomware or malware is suspected

If unsure whether something is an incident, report it anyway.

### How to report

Incidents should be reported through the company’s incident reporting channel, which may include:
- incident reporting email
- incident management system
- direct message to IT or security
- notifying manager and IT
- emergency communication channel for critical incidents

The report should include:
- description of what happened
- when it happened
- systems involved
- data involved (if known)
- who discovered the incident
- any actions already taken
- whether the incident is ongoing

Employees should not delay reporting while trying to fix the issue themselves.

## Initial response

Once an incident is reported:
- the incident should be acknowledged
- severity should be assessed
- responsible person or team should be assigned
- immediate containment actions may be taken
- affected systems may be isolated
- access may be temporarily disabled
- backups may be checked
- stakeholders may be informed depending on severity

The priority is to:
1. stop the problem from getting worse
2. protect data and systems
3. restore operations
4. understand what happened
5. document the incident

## Containment and mitigation

Containment actions may include:
- disabling accounts
- blocking access
- shutting down systems
- isolating affected systems
- revoking credentials
- restoring from backup
- stopping data transfers
- notifying affected teams
- applying emergency fixes

Containment does not necessarily solve the root cause but prevents further damage.

## Incident resolution

After containment, the responsible team should:
- identify the root cause
- fix the issue
- restore systems
- restore data if necessary
- verify systems are working correctly
- verify access permissions are correct
- confirm incident is resolved

Resolution may take time depending on the incident.

## Incident documentation

All significant incidents should be documented.

Incident documentation should include:
- description of the incident
- timeline of events
- systems affected
- data affected
- actions taken
- root cause
- resolution
- lessons learned
- follow-up actions

Documentation helps prevent similar incidents in the future.

## Communication during incidents

Communication should be controlled and clear.

Depending on severity:
- internal teams should be informed
- management may be informed
- clients may need to be informed
- status updates may be provided
- communication should be coordinated and consistent

Employees should not communicate externally about incidents unless authorized.

## Incidents involving data

If an incident involves data exposure, data loss, or unauthorized access to data:
- the incident must be reported immediately
- access may need to be removed
- systems may need to be secured
- affected data should be identified
- the Data Handling Policy should be followed
- additional steps may be required depending on the type of data involved

## Lost or stolen devices

If a company device is lost or stolen:
- report immediately
- provide device details
- IT may remotely disable or wipe the device
- accounts may be disabled
- access may be revoked
- incident should be documented

Employees should not delay reporting lost devices.

## Responsibilities

### Employees and contractors

Employees and contractors must:
- report incidents immediately
- not hide incidents
- cooperate with incident response
- provide accurate information
- not attempt unauthorized fixes
- follow instructions during incident response

### Managers

Managers should:
- ensure incidents are reported
- help coordinate communication
- support incident response
- ensure business continuity where possible
- participate in incident review where needed

### IT and security

IT or security roles should:
- assess severity
- coordinate response
- perform containment
- restore systems
- investigate root cause
- document incidents
- implement preventive measures

## Common mistakes to avoid

Common incident management mistakes include:
- not reporting incidents quickly
- trying to fix incidents alone without reporting
- ignoring small security issues
- deleting logs or data before investigation
- not documenting incidents
- communicating incident details externally without approval
- assuming someone else reported the incident
- delaying reporting because the incident might be embarrassing
