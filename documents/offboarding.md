# Employee Offboarding Policy

## Purpose

This document defines the standard process for offboarding employees, contractors, and other personnel when their relationship with the company ends or when their access and responsibilities must be materially reduced.

The purpose of offboarding is to ensure that departures are handled in a consistent, secure, and operationally responsible way. A well-executed offboarding process protects company information, reduces operational disruption, supports continuity of work, and helps ensure that company-owned assets and access rights are properly recovered or terminated.

The offboarding process should ensure that:
- the final working day is clearly communicated
- digital access is removed in a timely manner
- company equipment is returned or otherwise accounted for
- ongoing work is handed over appropriately
- project, client, and internal ownership is reassigned where needed
- responsibilities between HR, the manager, IT, and the departing individual are clear

This document focuses on operational and access-related offboarding. It is not intended to replace legal, payroll, or employment-specific processes that may apply in parallel.

## Scope

This policy applies to:
- full-time employees
- part-time employees
- fixed-term employees
- interns
- contractors and consultants with company access or company-issued assets
- any individual whose departure requires account removal, work handover, or company asset recovery

This policy applies when:
- a person resigns
- a contract ends
- a temporary engagement ends
- the company terminates employment or engagement
- a role ends in a way that requires removal of access and return of assets

This policy covers:
- internal communication of the departure
- preparation for the final working day
- digital access removal coordination
- work handover and ownership reassignment
- recovery of company equipment
- closure of company-managed responsibilities

This policy does not define in detail:
- payroll settlement
- severance or legal termination requirements
- detailed data retention rules
- immigration or visa handling
- benefits continuation
- legal document wording for terminations
- disciplinary procedures

Those items may be governed by HR, legal counsel, local law, or separate internal procedures.

## Offboarding principles

The offboarding process should follow several core principles.

### Timeliness

Offboarding actions should be planned early enough that key tasks are not left until after the departure date. Delays create avoidable risk, especially for system access, client work continuity, and company asset recovery.

### Least disruption

Where possible, work should be handed over in a structured manner so that the departure does not unnecessarily block clients, projects, or internal operations.

### Security by default

Departing personnel should not retain access longer than necessary. Access removal should happen in accordance with the Security and Access Provisioning Policy and should be coordinated with the confirmed final working day.

### Clear ownership

Every major offboarding step should have a clear owner. If ownership is unclear, the default assumption should not be that “someone else is handling it.”

### Documentation

Important handover items, asset returns, and completion steps should be documented in a reasonable way. The company should be able to determine whether offboarding was actually completed or only assumed to be completed.

## Trigger for offboarding

The offboarding process begins once HR confirms the employee’s or contractor’s final working day.

In some cases, preparation may begin earlier once a departure is known, but the formal trigger for coordinated operational offboarding is confirmation of the final working day and communication to the relevant internal parties.

No offboarding plan should rely only on informal assumptions where the departure has not been confirmed.

## Roles and responsibilities

### HR responsibilities

HR is responsible for:
- recording the termination or contract end date
- communicating the confirmed final working day to the manager and IT
- coordinating exit-related documentation where applicable
- ensuring that the operational offboarding process is triggered in time
- helping clarify whether the departure is voluntary, scheduled, urgent, or otherwise operationally sensitive when that affects timing

HR is not solely responsible for technical account removal, equipment collection, or work handover, but HR plays a key coordination role.

### Manager responsibilities

The departing person’s manager is responsible for ensuring that business continuity and work ownership are addressed.

The manager must:
- review ongoing work and active responsibilities
- identify projects, clients, systems, and tasks affected by the departure
- reassign ownership where needed
- identify systems that require access removal
- ensure company assets are collected or return arrangements are confirmed
- determine whether any client-specific access or responsibilities need separate closure
- ensure that handover expectations are communicated clearly
- confirm whether there are any unusual timing requirements for access removal or transition

The manager should not assume that IT automatically knows all systems the person had access to, especially where project-specific or client-specific systems are involved.

### IT responsibilities

IT is responsible for implementing technical offboarding actions within the systems and environments under its administration or control.

This typically includes:
- disabling accounts
- disabling email
- disabling VPN
- disabling internal tools and collaboration access
- removing access from standard company-managed systems
- assisting with identification of assigned devices where applicable

IT should carry out access removal according to the confirmed timeline and relevant approvals. In higher-risk or urgent cases, earlier access removal may be required.

### Departing employee or contractor responsibilities

The departing individual is expected to:
- cooperate with the offboarding process
- return company-owned equipment
- support handover of current work where reasonably requested
- ensure important work information is not left only in personal notes or unmanaged locations
- stop using company systems after their authorized access period ends

The departing individual must not:
- delete important company information outside approved process
- move company documents into personal storage
- retain confidential information after departure where not permitted
- share credentials or attempt to preserve access after departure

## Offboarding timeline

### Stage 1: Departure confirmation

Once HR confirms the final working day, the manager and IT should be informed promptly.

At this point, the manager should begin reviewing:
- active work
- project responsibilities
- client-facing commitments
- system access footprint
- equipment assigned to the individual
- key handover dependencies

### Stage 2: Planning and preparation

Before the final working day, the manager should prepare a handover and closure plan appropriate to the role.

Depending on the role, this may include:
- listing ongoing tasks and their status
- identifying replacement owners
- collecting open action items
- identifying client-facing responsibilities
- documenting important contacts
- confirming where key files and documents are stored
- identifying any special system access that must be removed

Where the person has broad responsibilities, this planning should begin well before the last day.

### Stage 3: Final working day actions

By or on the final working day, the company should ensure that:
- work handover has been completed as far as reasonably possible
- equipment return is completed or a documented return arrangement exists
- access removal instructions have been executed according to schedule
- responsibility for active work has been reassigned
- the relevant internal stakeholders know the person has left or is leaving

Unless otherwise specified by internal procedure, digital access should be removed by the end of the final working day.

### Stage 4: Completion confirmation

The offboarding process should not be considered complete simply because the person has stopped working.

Completion should be confirmed only once:
- equipment return is confirmed
- digital access is removed
- work handover is completed or formally transferred
- outstanding ownership gaps are resolved
- any required client or internal notifications are completed

## Work handover

A structured handover is one of the most important parts of offboarding.

The manager should ensure that before departure:
- current work items are listed
- key deadlines are known
- task ownership is reassigned
- important documents are stored in accessible company-managed locations
- context needed to continue the work is available to the replacement owner or team

For some roles, the handover may be minimal. For others, it may require significant coordination. The level of detail should reflect the complexity and criticality of the role.

### Handover contents

A reasonable handover may include:
- summary of current responsibilities
- list of active projects
- status of major tasks
- unresolved blockers or risks
- key stakeholders or contacts
- location of important files
- known deadlines
- operational routines that someone else must take over

### Personal storage and undocumented knowledge

Managers should ensure that important business information is not left:
- only in the departing person’s notebook
- only in personal folders
- only in local, unmanaged device locations
- only in chat history without any summary or reference

This policy does not require perfect knowledge transfer in every case, but it does require reasonable effort to avoid preventable information loss.

## Equipment return

The employee or contractor must return company-owned equipment, including where applicable:
- laptop
- charger
- security badge
- access card
- mobile device
- hardware token
- any other assigned hardware or accessories

Equipment should be returned no later than the final working day unless another arrangement is approved.

If the individual works remotely, the manager and operations team should ensure there is a documented return arrangement, such as shipment, in-person drop-off, or another approved method.

### Recording return status

Where the company maintains an asset tracking process, equipment return should be recorded against that process.

If equipment cannot be returned on the final day, there should be a documented follow-up action rather than an informal assumption that it will be handled later.

### Damaged or missing equipment

If equipment is damaged, missing, or believed to be incomplete, the matter should be escalated through the company’s normal operational or HR process. This policy does not define financial or disciplinary handling of such cases.

## Digital access removal

Digital access removal is governed in conjunction with the Security and Access Provisioning Policy.

At minimum, when an individual leaves the company:
- email access must be disabled
- VPN access must be disabled
- access to internal tools must be disabled
- access to company-managed collaboration systems must be disabled
- access to critical systems may be removed earlier where necessary
- physical access should be revoked where applicable

The manager should help identify any additional systems not visible through standard IT processes, including:
- client systems
- project-specific tools
- shared workspaces
- internal dashboards or environments
- specialized engineering or finance platforms

### Timing expectations

Unless an exception applies, account deactivation should happen by the end of the final working day.

Earlier removal may be appropriate where:
- the departure is involuntary
- there is heightened security concern
- continued access is no longer operationally justified
- the role involves particularly sensitive systems

### Mailbox and communication continuity

This policy requires email access to be disabled, but it does not define detailed mailbox retention, forwarding, or reassignment rules. Where business continuity requires message handling after departure, that should be addressed through a separate approved internal process.

## Client and project-specific closure

Some departures affect client-facing work or systems that are not fully covered by standard internal offboarding.

The manager should determine whether any of the following are needed:
- notification to client stakeholders
- removal from client-managed systems
- reassignment of external meeting ownership
- update of project documentation
- change in escalation contacts
- replacement of named responsible persons in project files or tools

The company should not assume that client-specific access ends automatically just because internal access was removed.

## Knowledge transfer and documentation hygiene

Before departure, the manager should ensure:
- key documents are handed over
- passwords are not stored in personal locations
- ownership of ongoing tasks is reassigned
- important contacts and project status are documented
- critical files are stored in company-managed repositories or document systems

This does not mean every file must be reorganized before departure. It means essential business continuity information should not disappear with the person leaving.

## Special cases and exceptions

### Immediate departures
In some cases, the company may need to remove access immediately or earlier than normal. Examples include:
- involuntary termination
- serious policy concern
- urgent security need
- high-risk access footprint

In such cases, access removal may happen before the normal end-of-day timing, and handover may be handled differently.

### Contractors with limited scope
Some contractors may have very limited access and no company equipment. In those cases, offboarding may be lighter, but the company should still confirm:
- access removal
- closure of project responsibilities
- return of any assigned assets if applicable

### Long notice periods
Where a person remains employed for a longer transition period, the manager should still avoid delaying the handover until the very end. Handover should be staged where practical.

### Leave before departure
If a person is expected to be unavailable before the formal final day, the manager should plan around the actual last active working period, not only the official contract end date.

## Minimum completion criteria

The offboarding process is considered complete only after:
- equipment return is confirmed, or documented follow-up exists
- access is removed
- work handover is completed or formally reassigned
- active ownership gaps are addressed
- final responsibilities are no longer dependent on the departed person’s access

Stopping work alone is not sufficient to mark offboarding complete.

## Common mistakes to avoid

The following are common offboarding failures and should be avoided:
- assuming IT knows every system the person used
- waiting until after departure to identify ownership gaps
- forgetting client-specific tools or accesses
- marking offboarding complete before equipment is returned
- leaving key knowledge only in personal notes
- assuming that internal email disablement also closes all external responsibilities
- delaying access removal because the person “might need one last thing”
