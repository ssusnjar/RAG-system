# HR Onboarding Policy

## Purpose

This document describes the standard onboarding process for new employees and contractors who require access to company systems, equipment, and internal documentation. The purpose of onboarding is to ensure that each new joiner can begin work in a structured, secure, and operationally effective manner from their first working day.

A successful onboarding process should ensure that:
- the individual is correctly registered in internal systems
- required equipment and accounts are prepared on time
- the hiring manager and relevant internal teams understand their responsibilities
- mandatory security and compliance steps are completed
- the employee has sufficient context, access, and support to begin contributing during the first week

This policy is intended to reduce delays, prevent access issues, and ensure consistency across teams.

## Scope

This policy applies to:
- full-time employees
- part-time employees
- fixed-term employees
- contractors and consultants who require company-managed accounts or equipment

This policy applies from the moment HR confirms that a signed contract or approved engagement document has been received.

This policy does not define:
- payroll processing details
- employee benefits
- probation period rules
- compensation review processes
- detailed information security controls beyond onboarding-specific requirements
- travel booking rules
- expense reimbursement rules

Those topics are covered by separate processes or policies where applicable.

## Roles and responsibilities

### HR responsibilities

HR is responsible for initiating onboarding once all required employment or engagement documents have been received. HR must:
- create the employee or contractor record in the HR system
- confirm the legal name of the joiner
- confirm start date
- confirm employment type or contract type
- confirm reporting manager
- confirm work location
- confirm whether the person is remote, hybrid, or office-based
- notify the hiring manager that onboarding has formally started
- notify IT of the planned start date when onboarding prerequisites are met

HR is also responsible for ensuring that core employment documentation is completed before the first working day, to the extent required by local practice.

### Hiring manager responsibilities

The hiring manager is responsible for ensuring that the new joiner can be operationally productive. At least 3 business days before the start date, the hiring manager must submit or confirm:
- required systems access request
- laptop requirement
- software and tooling request
- team assignment
- reporting line confirmation
- buddy assignment for the first 2 weeks
- role-specific documentation or training needs
- any project-specific access requirements already known

The hiring manager is also responsible for checking that requests are realistic and limited to business need. Managers should not request elevated or production-level access by default unless specifically justified and separately approved.

### IT responsibilities

IT is responsible for preparing the technical environment for the new joiner based on approved requests and standard role profiles. IT typically prepares:
- company email account
- communication tools
- laptop
- document storage access
- issue tracking tool access
- standard collaboration tools
- baseline security settings on issued devices

IT does not automatically grant production access during onboarding. Production or privileged access requires a separate request and approval by the relevant system owner or responsible approver.

### New joiner responsibilities

The new employee or contractor is expected to:
- complete account activation steps when instructed
- activate multi-factor authentication during the first week
- attend mandatory onboarding sessions
- complete required security awareness training
- review team documentation shared by the manager or buddy
- raise issues early if access or equipment is missing

## Onboarding timeline

### Stage 1: Onboarding trigger

The onboarding process begins once HR confirms receipt of:
- signed employment contract, or
- approved contractor engagement documentation

No onboarding should begin informally based only on verbal confirmation unless an exceptional internal approval has been granted.

### Stage 2: Pre-start preparation

Once onboarding is triggered, HR creates the employee record and shares the required onboarding details with the hiring manager and IT.

At least 3 business days before the employee’s start date, the hiring manager should ensure that all standard requests have been submitted. If a laptop, special software, or uncommon access setup is needed, earlier notice is strongly recommended.

Where possible, IT should prepare accounts and equipment before the start date so that the new joiner can access core systems on day one.

### Stage 3: First working day

On the first working day, the new joiner should receive:
- laptop or other approved company equipment
- account credentials or account activation instructions
- onboarding schedule
- security training invitation
- introduction meeting with the manager
- introductions to the team and assigned buddy where applicable

If equipment delivery is delayed for a remote employee, the manager should ensure that the employee still receives a first-day plan and a temporary onboarding structure where possible.

### Stage 4: First week

During the first week, the new joiner must:
- activate multi-factor authentication
- complete mandatory security awareness training
- review internal documentation relevant to the team
- attend onboarding meetings
- confirm access to the required standard systems
- raise missing-access issues to the manager or IT

The hiring manager should schedule at least one check-in during the first week to confirm:
- equipment was received
- required standard access is working
- onboarding sessions were attended
- immediate blockers are known

### Stage 5: Early ramp-up period

During the first 2 weeks, the hiring manager and buddy should help the new joiner understand:
- team objectives
- primary tools and workflows
- documentation locations
- communication expectations
- escalation paths for operational or technical issues

This policy does not prescribe a detailed 30/60/90-day performance framework, but teams are encouraged to use one where appropriate.

## Standard access principles during onboarding

New joiners may receive standard access appropriate to their role, which commonly includes:
- email
- chat
- shared documents
- issue tracking tools
- internal knowledge base access
- standard collaboration systems

Access must follow least privilege and business need. Standard onboarding does not include:
- production access by default
- administrator permissions
- unrestricted access to sensitive financial or client data unless role-specific and approved
- shared accounts

If the role requires special or client-specific access, the relevant approvals should be requested through the appropriate process. Some access may only be granted after additional approvals, security review, or confirmation of project assignment.

## Equipment and software

The default equipment setup should align with the role profile where one exists. For example, engineering hires may require a different baseline tool set than finance or operations hires.

Where the manager requests non-standard software or hardware, the request should include a brief business justification.

If the employee is remote, equipment shipment should be planned early enough to avoid delays. If this is not possible, the manager and IT should coordinate a fallback plan.

The issuance of equipment should be recorded according to the company’s asset tracking process where such process exists.

## Mandatory training and compliance steps

All new joiners are expected to complete mandatory onboarding-related training assigned to them. This generally includes:
- security awareness training
- review of relevant team or internal process documentation
- any required compliance acknowledgments assigned at onboarding

Completion of mandatory training should happen during the first week unless a different deadline is specified internally.

Managers are responsible for following up if required training has not been completed in a reasonable timeframe.

## Delays, exceptions, and edge cases

### Missing information before start
If HR or IT does not receive sufficient information before the start date, onboarding may be delayed or incomplete. In such cases, the manager should be informed as early as possible.

### Urgent hires
For urgent hires, parts of the onboarding process may be accelerated. However, security requirements such as named accounts, least privilege, and multi-factor authentication should not be bypassed.

### Contractors with limited scope
Some contractors may require only a subset of standard onboarding steps if they do not receive company equipment or broad system access. Even in those cases, the manager must still define required access and business need.

### Delayed access to restricted systems
Some systems, particularly production or client-related systems, may require separate approvals or reviews. Delayed access to those systems should not be treated as an onboarding failure if the standard onboarding process was completed correctly.

## Success criteria for onboarding completion

Operational onboarding is considered substantially complete when:
- the new joiner has received required equipment or an approved interim arrangement
- standard accounts are active
- multi-factor authentication is enabled
- mandatory onboarding tasks have been assigned or completed
- the manager has completed the first-week check-in
- the employee can access the systems needed for initial work

This does not mean all role-specific access, project-specific permissions, or advanced tooling must already be in place.
