# Expense Reimbursement and Approval Policy

## Purpose

This document defines the rules for submitting, approving, and reimbursing business-related expenses incurred by employees and eligible contractors on behalf of the company.

The purpose of this policy is to ensure that expenses are:
- legitimate and business-related
- approved at the appropriate level
- supported by sufficient documentation
- processed consistently across teams
- reimbursed in a predictable and auditable manner

A good expense process should allow employees to recover valid business costs without unnecessary friction while also ensuring that the company maintains financial control and avoids reimbursing personal, unapproved, or poorly documented spending.

This policy is intended to help employees, managers, Finance, and approvers understand what can be claimed, how claims should be submitted, and when additional approval is required.

## Scope

This policy applies to:
- full-time employees
- part-time employees
- fixed-term employees
- interns where reimbursement is permitted by company practice
- contractors or consultants only where their engagement terms explicitly allow expense reimbursement

This policy applies to reimbursable business expenses paid directly by an individual and later submitted for reimbursement.

This policy covers:
- submission requirements
- approval thresholds
- supporting documentation
- reimbursement timelines
- non-reimbursable examples
- special handling notes for travel-related spending

This policy does not define:
- corporate card usage rules
- procurement or purchase order procedures
- vendor contracting rules
- detailed accounting treatment of every expense category
- daily allowance values
- tax treatment for all jurisdictions
- travel booking rules in full
- relocation or immigration support expenses

Those topics may be handled by Finance, procurement, the Travel Policy, or separate internal procedures.

## Core principles

The following principles apply to all expense submissions.

### Business purpose

All reimbursable expenses must have a clear business purpose. A valid expense should be directly connected to company work, client delivery, business travel, approved operational activity, or another legitimate company need.

An expense is not reimbursable simply because it occurred during working hours or was paid by an employee.

### Reasonableness

Expenses should be reasonable in amount and proportionate to the underlying business need. Employees are expected to exercise practical judgment and avoid unnecessary or excessive spending.

Reasonableness should be evaluated in context, including:
- the purpose of the expense
- available alternatives
- urgency
- location
- company norms
- whether prior approval was required or obtained

### Documentation

Claims must be supported by sufficient documentation to allow review and reimbursement. In general, the company must be able to understand:
- what was purchased
- when it was purchased
- from whom
- for what business purpose
- for how much
- who needs to approve it

### Timeliness

Expense claims should be submitted promptly. Late claims are more difficult to verify, delay closing of reporting periods, and increase the risk of missing documentation.

### Approval before reimbursement

Reimbursement does not depend only on whether a receipt exists. The expense must also meet the policy requirements, including any prior approval requirements and approval thresholds.

## Eligible expense categories

The company may reimburse business-related expenses such as:
- local transportation for approved business purposes
- meals connected to approved business travel or approved business meetings
- accommodation for approved business travel
- approved software or small operational purchases made on behalf of the company
- client-related costs where approved and properly documented
- other business expenses reasonably incurred in support of company work

Eligibility depends on the expense being:
- necessary
- reasonable
- properly documented
- compliant with any relevant approval requirements
- not otherwise excluded by this policy or another policy

An expense being in a normally reimbursable category does not guarantee reimbursement if the specific spend was unnecessary, excessive, outside approval rules, or inadequately documented.

## Non-reimbursable examples

The company will generally not reimburse:
- personal entertainment
- personal purchases
- traffic fines
- parking fines or other penalties
- unapproved first-class travel
- premium upgrades that were not approved
- missing-receipt claims unless explicitly approved by Finance
- expenses submitted without a clear business purpose
- costs that should have been handled through procurement or a company purchasing channel when that requirement applied
- duplicate claims
- expenses incurred for another person without explanation or approval
- personal subscriptions or equipment purchases without prior approval

This list is illustrative, not exhaustive. Finance or the approver may reject an expense that is not explicitly listed above if it is clearly personal, unsupported, non-compliant, or unreasonable.

## Required documentation

Each expense claim should include:
- date of expense
- vendor or merchant name
- business purpose
- amount
- currency if not in local currency
- receipt or invoice

Where relevant, the claim should also include:
- project or client reference
- names of attendees for a business meal or meeting
- travel context
- approval reference if pre-approval was required
- justification for any unusual cost level

Claims missing core documentation may be rejected or returned for clarification.

### Receipts and invoices

A receipt or invoice should normally be attached for every claim. A clear digital copy is acceptable where permitted by internal process.

If the receipt is missing, the expense is generally not reimbursable unless Finance explicitly approves an exception. The existence of a bank transaction alone does not automatically replace the need for supporting documentation.

## Submission deadline

Expense claims must be submitted within 15 calendar days of the expense date.

This deadline applies unless:
- a different timeline is explicitly stated by another policy for a specific type of expense, or
- Finance grants an exception due to a justified circumstance

Late submissions may be rejected, delayed, or escalated for additional review.

Submitting within the deadline does not guarantee reimbursement if other requirements are not met.

## Approval thresholds

The following approval thresholds apply to individual expense claims unless a more specific internal rule exists for the type of expense.

- Expenses up to 100 EUR require employee submission and direct manager approval.
- Expenses above 100 EUR and up to 1,000 EUR require direct manager approval.
- Expenses above 1,000 EUR require both direct manager approval and Finance approval.
- Expenses above 5,000 EUR require direct manager approval, Finance approval, and department head approval.

These thresholds apply to the amount of the claim being submitted. Where a larger spend is artificially split into multiple smaller claims to avoid approval rules, the claims may be treated together for review purposes.

### Interpretation notes

For practical interpretation:
- a claim of 100 EUR falls into the first threshold
- a claim of 1,000 EUR falls into the second threshold
- a claim of 1,000.01 EUR falls into the third threshold
- a claim of 5,000 EUR falls into the third threshold
- a claim above 5,000 EUR requires the additional department head approval

Where there is uncertainty about how a claim should be treated, the stricter approval path may be applied until clarified.

## Pre-approval expectations

Some expenses require judgment before the spend happens, not only after the fact.

Employees should seek pre-approval where:
- the expected spend is unusually high
- the expense is outside normal recurring business practice
- the purchase involves premium services
- there is uncertainty whether the item is reimbursable
- the spend is travel-related and subject to the Travel Policy
- the spend may be better handled through procurement or direct company payment

Pre-approval is especially important where the employee expects that the expense may later be challenged as unusual, premium, or avoidable.

Pre-approval does not replace the need to submit the claim with full documentation afterward.

## Travel-related expenses

Travel-related expenses must comply with both this policy and the Travel Policy.

This means:
- travel expenses still need receipts or invoices
- travel expenses still need to be submitted within the required deadline
- approval thresholds in this policy still apply where relevant
- travel bookings or travel class choices may be restricted by the Travel Policy

For example:
- an approved business trip does not automatically make every travel-related purchase reimbursable
- premium travel choices may still be rejected if not properly approved
- unapproved premium travel bookings may be partially or fully rejected for reimbursement

Where the Travel Policy and this policy are both relevant, both must be satisfied.

## Meals and hospitality

Business meals may be reimbursable where there is a clear business purpose, such as:
- approved travel
- internal working sessions where reimbursement is normal company practice
- client meetings or business development activities where appropriate

Meal claims should be reasonable and should include enough context to understand:
- who attended
- why the meal was business-related
- whether the cost was proportionate

Purely personal meals are not reimbursable simply because they occurred during a working day.
